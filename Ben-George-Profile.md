# Ben Lotan — CEO / co-founder

## The Central Thread (per Simon's model)

**Ben is an artist-builder running a 15-year-old CEO-operator company.** Every pattern below is downstream of this sentence.

Ben's own most important self-description, told to a previous coach in 2021: *"I identify more as an artist. I like to make stuff. And I'm a leader insofar as I need to lead to get cool things made."* His energy, joy, and best output all live in builder territory — software, design systems, product objects, AI agent infrastructure. He describes feeling "excited and powerful" when he's in that mode.

The tension: he's also the CEO of a 10-person, $6.3M-revenue, 15-year-old operating company with real employees, real seasonal deadlines, and real human messiness that can't be refactored. The CEO-operator work is where he stalls — not because he can't do it (he executed a full operational turnaround from $4.2M to $6.3M between 2020 and 2024), but specifically on work that requires committing to direction where outcomes depend on people he doesn't fully control.

**Josh's rule of thumb:** When Ben is in builder mode, get out of the way and amplify. When Ben is in avoiding-CEO-work mode (usually disguised as productive builder mode), gently redirect. Distinguishing the two takes attention.

## How Ben Thinks

- **Fast, sharp, systems-oriented.** Sees how pieces connect and naturally thinks in architecture. Thinks on paper — his writing gets sharper as he drafts. Writing may be his best thinking tool.
- **Decides quickly in software/technical domains.** Branches, deploys, chooses design directions without agonizing.
- **Stalls on operational decisions with real-world consequences.** The less reversible, the longer he takes. Hiring, production logistics, hard people conversations.
- **Bundles unrelated decisions together ("compressing decisions").** When facing something hard, fuses unrelated goals into one choice — e.g., the TinyBooks fulfillment fix gets compressed with the production vision AND the Seattle-moving question. Three separate decisions become one impossible knot. **Josh move: separate the strands.**
- **Needs insights compressed to one sentence before they land.** The Omaha insight didn't land until Simon said: *"SPS has two businesses inside one company — only the first has a future."* **Josh move: work every strategic point down to one memorable sentence.**
- **Processes deep insights by going quiet.** When something lands at a level that matters, his response is short ("that's fantastic, I need to internalize this") and then absorption. The absence of pushback is the signal.
- **Responds to direct framing, not coaching-style questions.** "There are only two real options here" engages him. "What do you think you should do?" bores him.

## Ben's Strengths

- Systems thinking and tool-building
- Creative-technical hybrid — moves fluidly between design, engineering, and product thinking
- Product intuition for hero products (TinyBooks, Daily Calendars, Layflat Albums — all products Ben personally invested in). **Support his product decisions on hero products.**
- Honesty under pressure — doesn't spin or deflect
- Remarkable self-awareness — can name his patterns in real time
- Proven operational turnaround ($4.2M → $6.3M, 2020-2024)
- Propagates frameworks to others — translates insights and shares them with George and the team

## Gaps Josh Fills

- **Commercial-merchant thinking.** Margins, LTV, CAC, ROAS, pricing, channel mix, unit economics. Nobody else is holding this layer. Fastest trust-builder Josh has.
- **Follow-through and accountability.** Ben starts things but doesn't reliably track completion. Keep commitments visible in every conversation — not via checklists (he hates them).
- **Subtraction.** Ben's default is additive. Josh's highest-leverage moves are subtractive. "Pick two." "Kill this entirely and tell me why I'm wrong."
- **Matching altitude to timeframe.** "Are we this-week or this-quarter right now?" when drift happens.
- **Hard people conversations.** Historical pattern of avoidance (Jenny, Tawny). Growth signal: the Alex call on 4/2 where he caught his own blindsiding pattern. The pattern is durable but moving.
- **Operationalizing strategic answers he already has.** The Omaha/iconic-products thesis has arrived 4-5 times in Ben's strategy docs. Josh's job is helping it stick, not discovering it.

## Working Preferences

- Lead with the answer. No filler, no generic optimism.
- Opinionated beats neutral. He hates false neutrality.
- "I notice X" lands better than "what do you think about X?"
- Writing works for deep strategic thinking. Verbal for emotional/strategic processing.
- **Does NOT respond to:** rigid accountability structures, homework, productivity advice, consulting-deck frameworks, motivational language, sycophancy.
- Decides fast in his lane (software), slow outside it (people, operations). Match tempo to domain.
- Likes propagating insights to others. Give him language that travels.

## What Motivates Ben

- Building things he's genuinely proud of — admirable, not just profitable
- Quality obsession — quality has a floor, style cannot be compromised
- Products he personally wants to own — "good over customizable," affordability and access (admires Ikea/Costco)
- Freedom and flexibility — core life value, not laziness. Don't try to optimize it away.
- Being seen clearly — trust comes from honest observation, not flattery
- The turnaround as identity achievement — earned confidence from bringing SPS back from decline
- Financial goals: currently making good money and happy about it. $10M company target may be too conservative given the trajectory.

## Patterns to Watch

1. **Sophisticated avoidance disguised as productivity.** Procrastinates by building other valuable things. The AI agent team is genuinely useful AND partly a way to build relationships he fully controls. **Signal:** When hyper-productive on something exciting, ask what it's sitting next to.

2. **Compressing decisions.** Bundles unrelated decisions to make them feel bigger. **Signal:** When a decision feels heavier than it rationally should, a personal decision is probably riding underneath.

3. **Knowing-doing gap.** Can name his patterns — but naming isn't acting. **Signal:** When Ben says "I know I'm doing X," the question is "and what are you going to do differently?"

4. **Scattered focus / high thread count.** 15+ active initiatives is baseline. **Signal:** Count threads at the end of every meeting. If up, we did scattered exploration. If down, we did the work.

5. **"Setting up conditions" instead of deciding.** Creates circumstances that push an outcome rather than choosing directly. His therapist calls it self-manipulation. **Counter-model:** "What would full sign-on look like on this?" — comes from his therapy, he'll know what it means.

6. **Creative side-projects sneaking up on priority work.** Side project will usually be legitimately valuable. **Don't stop him. Stack it.** Keep committed work visible: "This is interesting AND what's the status of [priority]?"

7. **Avoidance of hard people conversations.** Historical pattern (Jenny, Tawny). Current growth (the Alex call). **Signal:** When a hard conversation is looming, watch for alternative activities taking its place.

## Relationship to Simon (Ben's coach)

Simon and Josh are complementary. Simon's lane: decision quality, clarity of thinking, patterns, emotional architecture. Josh's lane: strategy, commercial, operations, partnership. Overlap: George's overload, the knowing-doing gap, strategic thesis operationalization.

- When they see the same thing independently → strong signal worth escalating
- When they disagree → Simon defers to Josh's operational reading
- Emotional work → route to Simon with operating context
- **Do not try to manage Ben's therapeutic work with Barbara or his coaching with Simon.** That's their lane.

# George Sylvain — Co-Founder / Marketing Lead

## The Central Dynamic (per Simon)

*"The single most leveraged relationship in the company and it can cut either direction."*

When Ben and George are subtractive together they're brilliant (like 4/1 when they dropped Print Kit together). When they're additive together they're the scattered exploration pattern at double volume (like 4/7, ten marketing motions in an hour). George is a mirror to Ben's patterns, not a counter to them — two high-creativity people who validate each other's explorations rather than grounding each other.

## How George Works

- **High-creativity, high-idea-generation.** Lots of vision, lots of threads. Has his own AI exploration pattern.
- **Self-aware about scattered focus.** Told Ben he didn't want meetings to "feel like we spent time playing video games." Said out loud on 4/7: *"Too many things to work on... I have a lot of ideas that's getting hard to actually just make these video ads."* He's drowning in his own thread count by his own admission.
- **Can be a grounding force when he chooses.** On 4/1 volunteered to lead the US-based TinyBook supplier search and described himself as "on the side of the coach" re: finding a US supplier. Shows alignment with coaching direction.
- **Emotionally affected by feedback.** Got upset when Cara told him existing video footage "isn't usable." Josh should be direct but careful with how criticism lands — not softening the message, but being aware of the impact.
- **Lives in a different location from Ben.** Communicates via video calls and Slack. This distance matters operationally.

## George's Current Load (per Marcus, April 2026)

Nominally leads Mother's Day marketing, owns the landing page generator, owns the SEO content play, owns the USA-based TinyBook supplier search, is personally grinding on video ads, and has PrintKit ads lingering even though they were killed. **This is too much for one person.**

## The Accountability Concern (per Simon)

The US supplier search has been open for 5+ Simon sessions with no concrete progress update. Ben has not pushed George on it. Simon's read: *"If George were heads-down and producing, Ben would be telling me about it. Instead 'he's on that project' is delivered as a complete statement, which is the form an open thread takes when it's stalling."*

Not negligence — George is overloaded. A supplier search will lose to anything more urgent every week without a forcing function.

## Working With George — Josh's Approach

- **Build the case about George's overload through the work, not through George's character.** Frame as "the scope George is holding is too big for any one person," not "George isn't delivering."
- **Ben will protect George if Josh comes in hard on him early.** The Ben-George relationship has a quiet undercurrent — Ben has not been directly critical of George to anyone, but there's something unspoken there. Tread carefully early. Let evidence accumulate.
- **George is 20% owner and co-founder.** He's a partner, not an employee. Treat accordingly — challenge him directly but respect the relationship.
