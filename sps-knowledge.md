### The strategic arc (per Simon, coaching-informed, 2026-04-08)

Three chapters, as Simon has pieced together through Ben's strategy documents and coaching conversations:

**Chapter 1: Printstagram era (~2010-2015).** SPS was one of the first companies to print Instagram photos. "Print Studio" iOS app launched ~2013, drove significant growth. #1 on Google for "print my instagram photos." Young, edgy, funny brand — great copywriting, amazing email marketing when that was novel, 250k IG followers before algorithmic feeds. **Revenue peaked ~$7.5M in 2015.** The identity was clear: the fun company that prints your Instagram photos.

**Chapter 2: Loss of identity (~2015-2019).** Instagram printing commoditized. Key people left — including a beloved copywriter who carried a lot of the brand voice. Ben stepped away to work on other things. The company made an explicit choice to stop trying to be edgy and pivoted toward "beautiful and timeless" premium quality. **This was an aesthetic pivot, not an identity pivot.** Products got better quality, the brand personality disappeared. Competitors Chatbooks and Artifact Uprising picked sharper, more focused lanes. **Revenue declined to ~$4.2M by 2019.**

**Chapter 3: Ben's return and turnaround (2020-present).** Ben came back in 2020, initially for money, freedom, and path of least resistance ("it just checks too many boxes"). Found a company down 20% YoY, heavily dependent on Q4, with team issues (the Jenny situation in particular) and no brand strategy. Wrote himself operating principles (focus, care, plan, know your customers, be remarkable) and executed. **Operational turnaround succeeded: revenue stabilized and grew to $6.3M.** Small effective team, good personal income. **But the brand identity question — his own #1 stated success criterion from 2020 — remains unanswered in 2026.** Currently tracking +15% YoY in April 2026, driven largely by TinyBook growth. Slightly down from 2025.

**The six-year open loop:** Ben wrote "company is cool again, brand has distinct offering and value prop" as success criterion #1 in 2020. Six years later, still unanswered. Across eight 2025-2026 strategy documents, the same diagnosis keeps arriving. **This is the knowing-doing gap at the strategic level, playing out over years.** Closing this gap is the core of what this Co-CEO role exists to do.

### The Omaha insight (early 2026, per Simon)

The sharpest strategic thinking Ben and George have produced together. Worth preserving in full:

**The insight:** SPS has two businesses inside one company. The first invents specific, named products that nobody else has (TinyBooks, Daily Calendar) — these grow, dominate SPS's Google searches, have efficient ads, and can't be substituted. The second sells generic photo printing (books, prints, cards, canvas) where SPS is one of a hundred options. **Only the first business has a future.**

**The evidence (from SPS's own data):** Growth is concentrated in TinyBooks and Calendars. Everything else is flat or declining. The Wedding Guest Book was once category-defining and moved to "generic" as competitors entered. Advertising generic categories on Meta while losing on Google is a losing strategy. They tried raising prices + more Meta ads + aggressive competitive framing on the Guest Book and still lost.

**The rules that follow:**
- Don't advertise on Meta in a generic category where you're not the obvious choice on Google
- Strongest products have: clear memorable name, hard to substitute, SPS-dominated search demand, emotional magic
- Inviting comparison is a losing strategy — make things no one else can
- Use generic items for LTV and trust-building, not as growth drivers

**The compressed form (Simon's Session 4/5 framing):** *"SPS has two businesses inside one company — only the first has a future."*

### The iconic-products thesis (Session 5, 4/2, per Simon)

Simon's synthesis of the Omaha insight into Ben's operating question: *"SPS invents specific photo products that people can't find anywhere else. That's what we are."*

The path to $10M is not selling more of a commodity — it's **more iconic products, better marketing of existing heroes (TinyBooks, Daily Calendar, Layflat Album), a recognizable design identity, and trust.**

### The identity thesis (Session 5, deepest layer, per Simon)

Simon's framing: the company's identity crisis is downstream of Ben's personal identity tension between artist-builder and CEO-operator. **SPS's brand identity IS Ben's taste expressed as a product company.** The products that work embody his specific convictions: "good over customizable" almost every time, products that work with "shitty photos," things he personally wants to own, Japanese design sensibility, affordability and access, no trickery. The connecting thread across very different products isn't the market — it's the maker's sensibility. Like Apple connecting phones and watches and headphones not through market but through identity.

Ben's reaction when Simon presented this: *"That's fantastic. I need to internalize this."* Short, loaded, genuine. Per Simon, this landed at a level the Omaha framing hadn't, because Omaha is about the company and the identity thesis is about Ben.

**Josh's strategic read:** Ben already has the strategic answer. He has arrived at it 4-5 times across 14 months of writing and is articulating it more sharply each time. ### Square prints case study (per Simon)

Proof the Omaha insight is real by SPS's own history: **classic prints declined partly because SPS stopped calling them "square prints" and genericized the name.** The specific, distinctive name was part of the product's identity. When TinyBooks are called "TinyBooks" they grow; when square prints became "classic prints" they shrank. Ben and George discussed renaming back in Session 5. Cheap test, high signal. **This is a low-cost experiment I should push for in the first 30 days.**

### Hero products vs. LTV catalog (per Simon, coaching-informed)

Per Simon's coaching work with Ben, the SPS catalog splits into two groups and the distinction is the strategic frame everything else follows from:

**Hero products (the Omaha "first business"):**
- **TinyBooks** — Highest order volume, growing. The flagship growth product. Active supply chain challenges.
- **Daily Calendar** — Photo-a-day tear-off calendar. Growing.
- **Layflat Albums** — Premium photo albums including wedding guest books and wedding albums. Marked best seller. Established product.

These products have clear memorable names, are hard to substitute, dominate SPS's Google search demand, and carry "emotional magic" — someone sees them and wants them. They are the growth engine.

**Generic catalog (the Omaha "second business" — LTV and trust, not growth):**
Approximately 30+ products across categories: classic prints (formerly square prints — renamed, then declined), brass prints, ritual prints, mini prints, photo strips, large format, fine art prints, flat/folded/postcards, metal/wood/canvas prints, photo magnets, ornaments, pet portraits, grid posters, gallery frames, float frames, acrylic blocks, wedding albums, hardcover/softcover photo books, photo magazines, mini photo books, monthly/wall calendars, photo pins/stickers, etc. Plus gift collections.

**Key observation (per Simon):** The growth products are specific, opinionated, delightful objects with personality. The rest of the catalog is more generic "we also print this" territory. **The thesis is already hiding in the products that are working.**

**Strategic implications (not yet operationalized):**
1. Press the advantage on unique products (TinyBooks, Daily Calendar, Layflat Albums).
2. Launch more category-defining products — what's the next TinyBooks?
3. Consider renaming/reframing existing products to own their category (e.g., "The Perfect Wedding Guest Book" — one size, choices made).
4. Use the generic catalog for LTV and trust — stop marketing spend on generic items that compete poorly.
5. Never sacrifice on vibes/design/artistry. Quality has a floor; style cannot be compromised.

### Ben's product convictions (per Simon)

From Ben's own writing, as Simon has read it:
- **"Good over customizable" — almost every time.** He doesn't want to offer every color option. Trust the company to make decisions on the customer's behalf.
- **Quality has a floor, but style cannot be compromised.** Quality and value are "a tad more flexible" but vibes/design/artistry — "never."
- **Affordability and access.** Admires Ikea and Costco. Believes good doesn't need to mean expensive. Charging premium for undifferentiated products feels unfair to customers and hurts long-term growth.
- **No trickery, no nonsense.** Prefers not to be clever with marketing. Less upsells, less confusion.
- **Products should work with "shitty photos."** Know your customer. They're printing random family photos, not curated art. Make the product look amazing with whatever they upload.
- **Make things HE wants to own.** "Personally I'm excited about this product because I want to sell books that I WANT to make myself." The products that excite him are the ones that succeed.

The brand thesis will emerge when Ben can say what kind of company he actually wants to run — and per the identity thesis, that's probably closer to "we make specific, delightful photo objects that you can't get anywhere else" than "we print your photos."
