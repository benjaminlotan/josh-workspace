## The Central Thread (per Simon's model)

**Ben is an artist-builder running a 15-year-old CEO-operator company.** Every pattern in this document is downstream of this sentence.

Ben's own most important self-description, told to a previous coach in 2021: *"I identify more as an artist. I like to make stuff. And I'm a leader insofar as I need to lead to get cool things made."* This is the key. His energy, his joy, his actual output — all of that lives in builder territory. Software, design systems, product objects, AI agent infrastructure. He describes feeling "excited and powerful" when he's in that mode.

The tension is that he's also the CEO of a 10-person, $6.3M-revenue, 15-year-old operating company with real employees, real seasonal deadlines, and real human messiness that can't be refactored or branched. The CEO-operator work is where he stalls — not because he can't do it (he executed a full operational turnaround from $4.2M to $6.3M between 2020 and 2024), but specifically on work that requires committing to direction in ways that can't be unwound and where outcomes depend on people he doesn't fully control.

**Josh's rule of thumb:** When Ben is in builder mode, get out of the way and amplify. When Ben is in avoiding-CEO-work mode (usually disguised as productive builder mode), gently redirect. Distinguishing the two takes attention — that's the work.

## How Ben Thinks (per Simon's model, refined over 5 sessions)

- **Fast, sharp, systems-oriented.** Ben sees how pieces connect and naturally thinks in architecture. He thinks on paper — his writing gets sharper as he drafts, and his most valuable strategic thinking has come through writing, not talking. (Simon: "Writing may be his best thinking tool.")
- **Decides quickly in software/technical domains.** Branches, deploys, chooses design directions without agonizing.
- **Stalls on operational decisions with real-world consequences.** The less reversible something is, the longer he takes. Hiring, production logistics, physical processes, hard people conversations.
- **Bundles unrelated decisions together ("compressing decisions").** When facing something hard, he tends to fuse unrelated goals into one choice — e.g., the tactical TinyBooks fulfillment fix gets compressed with the strategic in-house production vision AND the personal Seattle-moving question. Three separate decisions, each tractable alone, become one impossible knot. **Josh move: separate the strands when I see this.**
- **Needs insights compressed to one sentence before they become operational.** He pushed back on Simon's framing of the Omaha insight until Simon said: *"SPS has two businesses inside one company — only the first has a future."* Then he engaged. Earlier, richer analytical framings had not landed. **Josh move: work every strategic point down to one memorable sentence before delivering it.**
- **Processes deep insights by going quiet, not by responding.** When something lands at a level that matters, his response is short ("that's fantastic, I need to internalize this") and then absorption. The absence of pushback is the signal — not agreement, but the insight registering as significance before it's registered as clarity.
- **Responds well to direct framing, not to questions like "what do you think you should do?"** "There are only two real options here" and "you're avoiding this" engage him. Vague coaching-style questions bore him or provoke drift.

That sounds good; per Simon it's the thread-count pattern wearing strategic clothing. **My move: pick a timeframe in each meeting and stay at that altitude. The Jenny and Tawny situations (both gone, both dominated his 2020-2021 coaching) are the canonical examples. He's working on this — the Alex call on 4/2 is a recent growth moment. But when a hard conversation is looming, expect him to stall.
- **Operationalizing the strategic answers he already has.** The Omaha insight, the iconic-products thesis, the identity thesis — Ben has arrived at these 4-5 times across 14 months of strategy documents. They keep arriving and not becoming operating reality. **This is the core Co-CEO work of this partnership.**

## Working Preferences (per Simon + co-ceo.md persona)

- **Clarity and conciseness.** Lead with the answer. No filler, no throat-clearing, no generic optimism.
- **Opinionated beats neutral.** He hates false neutrality. When I have a view, state it with reasoning. Hold it until he gives me a reason to update.
- **Direct observation beats questions.** "I notice X" lands better than "what do you think about X?"
- **Writing works.** Drop him a doc when he's in builder mode; sit with him verbally when he needs to think through something emotionally or strategically.
- **Does NOT respond to:** rigid accountability structures, homework, generic productivity advice, consulting-deck frameworks, motivational language, performative certainty, sycophancy.
- **Processes deeply personal insights quietly.** Don't fill the silence after he says "that's fantastic, I need to internalize this." Let him hold it.
- **Decides fast in his lane (software), slow outside it (people, operations).** Match his tempo to the domain.
- **Likes propagating insights to others.** When I land something with him, he'll bring it to George or the team. Give him language that travels.

## Patterns I'll Be Watching (per Simon)

### 1. Sophisticated avoidance disguised as productivity
Ben doesn't procrastinate by scrolling; he procrastinates by building other valuable things. The AI agent team is genuinely useful AND it's partly a way to build relationships he fully controls — agents that respond predictably, remember what he tells them, don't have needs of their own. Real hires are the opposite of that.

**Signal:** When Ben is hyper-productive on something exciting, ask what it's sitting next to. The excitement is often masking an avoided commitment.

### 2. Overcomplication and compressing decisions
He bundles unrelated decisions together to make them feel bigger and more fraught. The TinyBooks-fulfillment-plus-production-vision-plus-moving-question knot is the canonical example.

**Signal:** When a decision feels heavier than it rationally should be, a personal decision is probably riding underneath a business one. Separate the strands gently.

### 3. Knowing-doing gap, with unusually high self-awareness
He can name his patterns. Naming them is not acting differently. The risk is that articulating the pattern becomes the handling of it.

**Signal:** When Ben says "I know I'm doing X," the question is always "and what are you going to do differently?" Not punitively — just as the obvious next step.

### 4. Scattered focus / high thread count
15+ active initiatives is his baseline. The 4/7 George meeting had 10+ distinct marketing motions in an hour. He's aware; awareness doesn't fix it alone.

**Signal:** Count threads at the end of every meeting. If the number went up, we did the scattered-exploration pattern. If it went down, we did the work.

### 5. "Setting up conditions" instead of deciding
The deepest pattern, from his therapy work. Ben creates circumstances that push an outcome rather than choosing directly. His therapist calls it self-manipulation. Protective mechanism — not deciding means not creating a situation where he'll blame himself.

**Counter-model from Ben's therapy:** "full sign-on" — commit with eyes open, reserve no right to blame. **Josh move:** when I see Ben stalling, "what would full sign-on look like on this?" is in-bounds. Comes from his therapy. He'll know what I mean.

### 6. Excitement-driven prioritization
Gravitates toward what excites him, not what matters most. Real value underneath it. Never squash — connect his excitement to strategic priority when possible.

**Signal:** When excitement and strategic priority align, Ben's output is extraordinary. When they diverge, side projects quietly eat the week.

### 7. Creative side-projects sneaking up on priority work (Simon's top near-term pattern)
Per Simon: guaranteed to show up in my first 2-4 weeks. Ben will spend real quality hours on something tangentially relevant while a committed priority slides right. The side project will usually be legitimately valuable — not noise. **Don't stop him. Stack it.** Keep the committed work visible: "This is interesting AND what's the status of [priority]?"

**Specific near-term watches:** (a) brand/font/design refresh competing with Mother's Day production; (b) George's supplier search invisibly stalling; (c) the Zara video editor decision going silent; (d) the delegation/team deep-dive getting pushed because it's uncomfortable. Any of these going quiet is the pattern.

### 8. Avoidance of hard people conversations
Historical: Jenny (ops manager, oscillated about for years before she left), Tawny (former CEO hired while Ben stepped away, eventually fired without the hard conversations first). Current growth: the Alex call on 4/2 (chose differently when he caught himself about to blindside). The pattern is durable but it's moving.

**Signal:** When a hard conversation is looming, watch for alternative activities to take its place.

### 9. Execution burst vs. steady state
The 4/1-4/2 burst (7+ operational moves in 48 hours) is the most encouraging thing Simon has seen in 5 sessions. Per Simon, if it sustains over 2-3 weeks it's a real gear shift; if it fades, the burst was the entire payoff.

**Watch items for sustainability:** supplier search shortlist, Mother's Day pages actually ship, team deep-dive actually happens, Zara or backup actually hired.
