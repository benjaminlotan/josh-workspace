02 From the Editor

Issue 01 · Spring 2026. A studio for the photographers, the camera nerds, and the people who still print the picture.

Social Print Studio has always been a friend with extraordinary taste. For fifteen years we have made objects out of pictures: tinybooks for the road, layflat albums for the wedding, gallery frames for the wall, photo strips for the fridge. The work has been quiet and the work has been good. What we needed was a wardrobe to match it — a typographic posture that says, without saying, that the people running the press care about the press.

This direction proposes that posture. It is editorial. It is patient. It borrows the front-matter cadence of a photo quarterly and the structural confidence of a well-edited book. It treats type as the most important photograph on the page, and it treats the photographs like the type — composed with intent, captioned with metadata, set on a grid that the eye trusts before the brain catches up.

The brand is not sacred. The soul is. We are keeping the warmth, the green, the cream, the rust at the edges. We are losing the bubbles, the shouting labels, the arch that has carried us. In their place: a real serif with optical sizes, a grotesk that knows when to be quiet, and monospace where it earns its keep. Our pictures deserve a magazine. This is the magazine.

Pairing

Hanken Grotesk · Body proof at 15px / 1.62

The body face is Hanken Grotesk, a contemporary grotesk with humanist warmth in its terminals and apertures. It is set here at 15 pixels with a line height of 1.62 — generous, but not so airy that the page loses the density of a real magazine. The grotesk is for body, captions, labels, navigation, and buttons. It does its job and steps out of the way.

Fraunces handles every display moment. We use its variable optical size axis aggressively: at 144 it is high contrast and beautifully modulated, at 32 it is patient and readable, at 9 it would still hold its shape in a footnote. The italic is the soul of the system — calligraphic, generous, nothing like a slanted roman. We use it for one word in a headline at a time, never for a whole sentence, never for a label.

JetBrains Mono carries metadata. Plate numbers, exposure data, timestamps, dimensions, and the dateline at the bottom of every spread. It is the third voice, used sparingly, but its presence is what tells the reader this is a magazine made by people who know what an EXIF string looks like.
